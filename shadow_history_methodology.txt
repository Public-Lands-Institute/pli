PUBLIC LANDS INSTITUTE — SHADOW HISTORY FIELD METHODOLOGY
Research protocol and writing guide for the shadow_history field.
Last updated 2026-04-02.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
DEFINITION
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The shadow_history field documents history that is verifiably true but
structurally underreported: events and conditions that do not appear in
standard park interpretive materials, tourism copy, or managing-agency
press releases. The field is not an editorial opinion about a site. It
is a factual record drawn from administrative, legal, scientific, and
archival sources.

The field is distinct from displacement_tenure, which focuses on the
legal and treaty history of how land passed from indigenous peoples to
the United States. Shadow history addresses what happened after — and
sometimes well before — the land entered public ownership, including
events that occurred under public management.

CATEGORIES OF INQUIRY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Research each new site against all of the following categories. Not all
categories will yield findings for every site. If nothing significant
is found after genuine search effort, the field may be left empty or
omitted.

1. SEGREGATED LABOR (CCC, WPA, prison labor)
   The Civilian Conservation Corps (1933-1942) operated racially
   segregated camps nationally, formally codified by CCC director
   Robert Fechner in summer 1935. All-Black companies were typically
   numbered in the 200s (e.g., Company 526) or 1500s (e.g., Company
   1505-C) and were often assigned to geographically isolated sites
   to reduce contact with white communities.

   Questions to answer:
   - Did CCC companies work this site? Which company numbers?
   - Were any companies racially segregated all-Black units?
   - Were Black enrollees subsequently barred from using the facilities
     they built, and if so, until when?
   - Was WPA labor used? Were workers paid equitably?
   - Was prison or convict labor used in development?

   Primary sources: Living New Deal (livingnewdeal.org), state
   historical CCC records, NPS administrative histories, camp rosters
   in National Archives Record Group 35.

2. FORCED RESIDENTIAL REMOVAL (non-indigenous)
   Many parks and preserves required the condemnation and removal of
   existing communities of farmers, miners, and rural residents.
   This most commonly occurred in national park and national forest
   formation (1890s-1940s) but also in state park assembly and
   reservoir construction.

   Questions to answer:
   - Were families or communities displaced through eminent domain
     to assemble the park?
   - Were condemned landowners compensated at fair market value?
   - Were named communities, towns, or neighborhoods destroyed?
   - Are remnant structures, cemeteries, or place names preserved
     within the park that document former occupation?
   - Were racial or class distinctions documented in who was
     displaced versus who was retained (e.g., as guides or laborers)?
   - Did large private landholders accelerate extraction (logging,
     mining, quarrying) between authorization and actual condemnation,
     extracting maximum value before government acquisition?

   EMINENT DOMAIN RECORDS — WHERE TO LOOK:
   Eminent domain/condemnation records are NOT in EPA or environmental
   databases. They live in entirely separate repositories:

   Federal district court records (PACER — pacer.gov):
     Federal condemnation suits (NPS, Army Corps, USFWS acquisitions)
     are filed as civil cases in the U.S. district court for the
     district where the land is located. Search by park name or
     "United States v. [acres/parcel description]."

   State court records:
     State park condemnations go through state common pleas or
     circuit courts. Search by county and approximate acquisition
     date.

   NPS Administrative Histories (npshistory.com):
     The "Land Acquisition" chapter is the most reliable single
     source for national park eminent domain. Often names specific
     communities, families, compensation disputes, and racial
     dimensions of who was displaced and who was not.

   Army Corps of Engineers Digital Library (usace.army.mil):
     Project environmental impact statements and land acquisition
     histories for Corps-managed lands. Search by project name
     under "Engineer Pamphlets" and "Project Histories."

   LOC Chronicling America (chroniclingamerica.loc.gov):
     Historical newspapers 1770-1963. Search for "[Site Name]
     eminent domain," "[Site Name] condemnation," or "[County]
     park acquisition." Local papers covered condemnation
     proceedings in detail, often naming families and amounts.

   County deed and probate records:
     Condemnation proceedings create deed transfers; contested
     valuations often produce probate or chancery court records.
     Available at county recorders and on Ancestry.com for many
     states.

   NOTE: Eminent domain research is most likely to require direct
   archival access (PACER, county recorder, state archives) rather
   than web search. If accessible records are exhausted without
   findings, document what was searched with a TODO note rather
   than assuming no condemnation occurred.

   Primary sources: NPS administrative histories (npshistory.com),
   Army Corps of Engineers project histories (USACE Digital Library),
   LOC Chronicling America, federal and state court records (PACER),
   county deed records.

3. INDUSTRIAL CONTAMINATION
   Many public lands were acquired specifically because prior
   industrial use made them unsuitable or unprofitable for private
   development. Industrial legacy can include active contamination
   affecting current visitors, or contamination in adjacent systems
   that affects the site hydrology.

   Questions to answer:
   - Is the site or an adjacent property listed on EPA's CERCLA
     (Superfund) National Priorities List?
   - Is there a Brownfield designation?
   - Does the site have legacy mining, quarrying, milling, or
     manufacturing history that left contaminated soil, water, or
     sediment?
   - Is the site's watershed affected by acid mine drainage, PCBs,
     organochlorine pesticides, or petroleum contamination?
   - Were there documented industrial accidents or spills on or
     adjacent to the site?

   Primary sources: EPA Cleanups in My Community
   (cleanups.epa.gov), EPA Superfund site profiles (cumulis.epa.gov),
   NOAA Damage Assessment and Restoration Program (darrp.noaa.gov),
   USGS water quality publications, state environmental agency records.

4. INSTITUTIONAL LABOR AND CONFINEMENT
   Some public lands incorporate or abut property formerly used by
   state institutions — psychiatric hospitals, reformatories, poor
   farms, or tuberculosis sanatoria — that operated self-sufficiency
   agricultural programs using unpaid institutional labor.

   Questions to answer:
   - Was the land or an adjacent parcel previously held by a state
     institution?
   - Did the institution use patient or inmate labor on the land?
   - Were patients, inmates, or residents buried on the property?
   - Was land transferred to public park use after institutional
     closure, and what conditions governed that transfer?

   Primary sources: State hospital and institutional histories
   (Asylum Projects: asylumprojects.org), state archives, county
   recorder records, Find A Grave (findagrave.com) for institutional
   cemeteries.

5. ARCHAEOLOGICAL EXTRACTION AND ANCESTRAL ERASURE
   Archaeological sites within public lands have a documented history
   of unauthorized excavation, institutional collection without
   community consent, and failure to repatriate remains under NAGPRA
   (Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act, 1990).

   Questions to answer:
   - Were archaeological excavations conducted at the site before
     professional standards and legal protections existed?
   - Who conducted excavations, and where did recovered material go?
   - Have skeletal remains or funerary objects been subject to
     NAGPRA consultation or repatriation?
   - Is there documented looting or unauthorized artifact removal?
   - Were human remains displayed publicly, and when was the
     practice ended?

   Primary sources: NAGPRA databases (National Park Service and
   individual institutions), Ohio History Connection and equivalent
   state archaeological surveys, institutional museum NAGPRA
   registers, Wikipedia for named mounds and site histories.

6. MILITARY USE AND APPROPRIATION
   Federal public lands are frequently subject to military training
   use, airspace restrictions, weapons testing, or outright
   annexation. This is especially common at national wildlife refuges
   and national forests.

   Questions to answer:
   - Has any portion of the site been used for military training,
     weapons testing, or airspace operations?
   - Have Department of Defense joint-use arrangements restricted
     public access to portions of the site?
   - Was the land ever considered for military annexation?
   - Are there unexploded ordnance concerns on the site?

   Primary sources: Defense Technical Information Center (DTIC),
   National Archives military records, managing agency NEPA
   documents, inspector general reports.

7. PRE-PARK INDUSTRIAL HISTORY
   Even sites with clean current environmental records often have
   significant industrial histories: quarrying, logging, mining,
   milling, or commercial agriculture that reshaped the landscape
   before public acquisition.

   Questions to answer:
   - What was the land used for before public acquisition?
   - Were there commercial operations (quarries, mills, mines) that
     employed workers under documented hazardous conditions?
   - Did the prior industrial use define the landscape the park
     now interprets (e.g., reforested farmland presented as
     "wilderness")?
   - Were there company towns or dependent communities on the site?

   Primary sources: State and local historical societies, managing
   agency cultural resource reports, railroad and industrial company
   histories, Sanborn fire insurance maps (LOC).

RESEARCH PROTOCOL
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Step 1: Identify the site's primary category
Before searching, determine which of the seven categories above is
most likely to yield findings for this type of site:

  Reservoir / Lake          -> Forced residential removal, contamination
  National Park             -> Forced residential removal, CCC labor,
                               archaeological extraction
  National Wildlife Refuge  -> Military use, CCC/WPA labor
  State Park (pre-WWII)     -> CCC labor, prior industrial history
  State Park (post-WWII)    -> Institutional land transfer, contamination
  Urban / municipal park    -> Industrial contamination, segregated access
  Private preserve          -> Archaeological extraction, prior use
  Mining / quarry site      -> Industrial contamination, labor history

Step 2: Query the primary archival silos
Issue targeted searches in these repositories:

  EPA Cleanups in My Community:
    cleanups.epa.gov
    Search by site name or address. Look for CERCLA/Superfund status,
    Brownfield designation, or corrective action records.

  NOAA Damage Assessment and Restoration Program:
    darrp.noaa.gov
    Covers industrial spill liability, wetland contamination, and
    natural resource damage assessments.

  NPS Administrative Histories:
    npshistory.com
    Search for the park name. Look for chapters on "Land Acquisition,"
    "Pre-Park Residents," or "CCC/WPA."

  Living New Deal:
    livingnewdeal.org
    Search by site name for CCC and WPA project records including
    company numbers, project types, and labor histories.

  USGS Publications Warehouse:
    pubs.usgs.gov
    Search for water quality studies, post-disaster assessments,
    and contamination studies by watershed or site name.

  LOC Chronicling America:
    chroniclingamerica.loc.gov
    Historical newspapers 1770-1963. Search for "[Site Name] eminent
    domain," "[Site Name] condemnation," "[County] CCC camp."

  Asylum Projects:
    asylumprojects.org
    Comprehensive database of state institutions. Search by county
    or institution name adjacent to the site.

  National Archives Record Group 35 (CCC):
    archives.gov
    CCC camp rosters, inspection reports, and company records.
    Identifies racial composition of companies by number.

Step 3: Filter results
Discard all of the following source types as insufficient for citation:
  - Travel blogs and tourism boards
  - "Things to do" listicles
  - Press releases from the managing agency
  - Wikipedia as a terminal source (use it to find primary sources)
  - Undated or uncredited web content

Accept:
  - Government agency publications and databases (EPA, NPS, USGS,
    USACE, state agencies)
  - Peer-reviewed academic publications
  - Named journalism with publication date and outlet
  - Court records and legal citations
  - Archival databases with traceable provenance (Find A Grave,
    Asylum Projects, Chronicling America)
  - Wikipedia articles with cited primary sources (follow the
    citations; cite the primary source, not Wikipedia)

WRITING GUIDE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The shadow_history field is a plain text string written in the same
register as the other PLI narrative fields. It appears on individual
site pages between displacement_tenure and ecology.

Tone: Deadpan. Report facts as facts. Do not characterize events as
"shameful," "tragic," "troubling," or "disturbing." The facts speak
for themselves. Do not use hedging language ("it is possible that,"
"may have been") unless the uncertainty is genuinely material.

Format: Clauses separated by semicolons. 2-5 sentences maximum.
Do not use headers, bullet points, or markdown within the field value.

Specificity: Always prefer the specific over the general. Name the
people, the companies, the dollar amounts, the dates, the court cases.

  Wrong: "The park used segregated labor during the New Deal era."
  Right: "CCC Company 526, an all-Black unit, improved over 2,000
          acres of the park from approximately 1934 to 1937; Black
          Ohioans were subsequently barred from visiting the facilities
          they built under formal state park segregation policies."

Never use em dashes or en dashes. Use "to" for ranges (1934 to 1937)
and semicolons for clause breaks.

Do not repeat information already in displacement_tenure. If
indigenous forced removal is in displacement_tenure, do not restate
it in shadow_history unless there is a distinct additional fact
(e.g., specific archaeological extraction of remains).

If genuine research effort yields nothing significant, leave the field
empty. Do not invent findings or speculate. Do not write "No
significant shadow history documented" as a field value — simply
omit the field or write an empty string.

Model entries:

  STRONG (multiple documented facts, named specifics):
    "CCC Companies 2875 and 2876, composed of African American
    enrollees, built the park's infrastructure in 1935 and were
    housed in a segregated camp selected for its distance from white
    communities; Black Texans were legally barred from entering the
    park they built until federal desegregation pressure in 1964;
    Texas Parks and Wildlife acknowledges that most of the canyon's
    archaeological sites have been picked over and many artifacts
    removed."

  ADEQUATE (single well-documented fact):
    "The Graniteville quarry operated beginning in 1869 as a company
    town with approximately 700 residents at peak; silica dust
    exposure in granite quarrying was a nationally documented
    occupational hazard by the 1890s, producing silicosis in quarry
    workers, though no site-specific injury records for Graniteville
    appear in accessible public records."

  TOO VAGUE (do not use):
    "The site has a complex industrial history and was used for
    commercial purposes before becoming a park."

BIBLIOGRAPHY MAINTENANCE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Every source used to populate or update a shadow_history field value
must be added to shadow_history_bibliography.txt before deploying.

Format:
  Site name (as a section header using the ━━━ rule convention)
  Source title — Publisher/Database
    Full URL

shadow_history_bibliography.txt is archived with each deploy in
Archive/YYYY-MM-DD/ alongside sites.json and the HTML files.

When updating an existing shadow_history field, add new sources to
the existing site section in the bibliography rather than creating
a duplicate section.

ADDING SHADOW HISTORY TO A NEW SITE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

When a new location is added to the site (see the ADDING A NEW
LOCATION protocol in CLAUDE.md), shadow_history research should be
conducted as part of the initial research phase using the protocol
above. The field should be populated or left empty before the first
deploy, not added as a follow-up.

For sites where research is genuinely inconclusive or requires access
to physical archives (county recorders, state archives, NPS FOIA
requests), document what was searched and what remains open with a
TODO prefix:

  "TODO: RESEARCH SHADOW HISTORY -- [what was searched, what
  repositories to check]"

TODO entries are visible in the generated HTML and flag the site for
future archival research.
