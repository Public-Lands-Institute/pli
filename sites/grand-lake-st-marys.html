Grand Lake St. Marys State Park

OH
Geological age
~430 Mya Late Silurian dolomite (Greenfield Dolomite, Salina Group); bedrock overlain by Pleistocene glacial ground moraine, outwash, and lake plain deposits from pre-Illinoian, Illinoian, and Wisconsinan glaciations.
Epoch
Late Silurian (Ludlovian); surface Pleistocene (Wisconsinan).
Native lands
Miami (Myaamia) and Shawnee (Šawanwaki); the St. Marys River (Miami-Illinois: Nameewa siipiiwi; Shawnee: Hotaawathiipi) exits the lake's eastern end and drains to the Maumee at Fort Wayne, the principal Miami capital at Kekionga (Kiihkayonki); Treaty of Greene Ville (August 3, 1795) ceded west-central Ohio, signed by Little Turtle (Miami war chief) and Blue Jacket (Shawnee); Treaty of Fort Wayne (September 30, 1809) forced Miami, Potawatomi, Delaware, and Eel River cession of ~3 million acres in Ohio and Indiana, excluding Shawnee from negotiations despite Shawnee presence on ceded lands, a principal grievance driving Tecumseh's confederation; Miami removed to Kansas via Treaty of the Forks of the Wabash (1840); Shawnee removed west under Indian Removal Act 1830.
Displacement & Tenure
Ceded via Treaty of Greene Ville (1795, Article 3); surrounding prairie entered private agricultural ownership after county organization; State of Ohio constructed Grand Lake St. Marys 1837-1845 as a feeder reservoir for the Miami and Erie Canal, flooding approximately 17,000 acres of lowland prairie; canal era ended with railroad competition by the 1900s; lake transferred to Ohio DNR management as Grand Lake St. Marys State Park.
Shadow History
Canal construction (1837-1845) employed 1,700 German and Irish immigrant laborers at 30 cents per day over eight years in malarial wetland conditions; local tradition holds that thousands of workers died from disease, accident, and exhaustion during construction and were buried at the lake site, a history absent from park interpretation; following the canal era, surrounding watershed was converted to intensive row crop and livestock agriculture, concentrating the highest farm animal density in Ohio within a 171 square mile drainage basin; 80.5% of watershed in row crop and pasture delivers chronic phosphorus loading from manure applications that fuels year-round cyanobacterial blooms; Planktothrix (microcystin-producing cyanobacteria, hepatotoxic) achieved year-round dominance in the lake; Ohio EPA detected microcystin at four times the WHO recreational threshold in May 2009; summer 2010 saw a complete lake closure, 8-9 documented human illnesses, multiple dog deaths, and livestock deaths from cyanotoxin exposure; a $3.4 million emergency alum treatment applied 2.6 million gallons to the lake; January 18, 2011, Ohio Department of Agriculture officially designated the Grand Lake St. Marys HUC-12 a distressed watershed, the first such designation in Ohio history, triggering a ban on winter manure and fertilizer application and additional livestock operator requirements; TMDL analysis determined tributary phosphorus loads must be reduced 80-99% during most seasons to achieve water quality standards; near-year-round cyanobacterial blooms have persisted through the present with microcystin levels regularly exceeding WHO thresholds.
Ecology
Shallow hypereutrophic reservoir (average depth 5-7 feet) with persistent severe harmful algal blooms; dominant plant communities include emergent cattail (Typha spp., approximately 65% of vegetated margins), sedges, rushes, water plantain, and bur-reed; submerged macrophytes reduced by turbidity and cyanobacterial shading; fish assemblage includes crappie, largemouth bass, bluegill, yellow perch, northern pike, and channel catfish among approximately 25-30 documented taxa; common carp degrade habitat through vegetation destruction and sediment disturbance; bald eagle nesting pairs documented around lake margins.
Hydrology
Ohio's largest inland lake (13,500 acres, 52-mile shoreline, average depth 5-7 feet), constructed as a feeder reservoir for the Miami and Erie Canal (1837-1845); St. Marys River exits the lake's eastern end, flowing northeast 101 miles to the Maumee River at Fort Wayne, Indiana; watershed 171 square miles with a split drainage, approximately 60% historically toward the Wabash system (Ohio River drainage) and 40% northeast toward the St. Marys/Maumee (Lake Erie drainage); nine feeder canals from surrounding drainages were constructed to supply the reservoir during canal operations.
