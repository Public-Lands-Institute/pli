# Public Lands Institute — Site Context

Generated 2026-05-19.

---

## Project Overview

Public Lands Institute (PLI) is a static photographic index of public and protected lands published CC0 Public Domain at publiclandsinstitute.net. All images, metadata, and generated HTML are freely reusable without attribution. The project pairs field photography with structured metadata — geological age, Indigenous land history, displacement and tenure, shadow history, ecology, and hydrology — treating each site as a subject with multiple layers of documented history, not just scenic landscape.

Current index: 45 sites, 9 states (OH, KY, IN, WV, TN, TX, OK, MO, MI), 363 images.

---

## Site Selection Logic

**Geographic concentration.** The collection is anchored in Ohio and the surrounding Midwest. Ohio sites span the full range of the state's ecological and geological character: glaciated lakeshore (Magee Marsh, Oak Openings), unglaciated Allegheny Plateau (Hocking Hills, Fort Hill, Flint Ridge, Red River Gorge), river corridor (Cuyahoga Valley, Caesar Creek, Shawnee Lookout), and the Appalachian Escarpment (Edge of Appalachia). Out-of-region sites document extended trips: Texas panhandle (Palo Duro, Caprock, Sea Rim), Oklahoma (Wichita Mountains, Charon's Garden), Missouri Ozarks (Johnson's Shut-Ins, Elephant Rocks), and Appalachian region (New River Gorge, Big South Fork, Mammoth Cave).

**Shadow history as curatorial logic.** A persistent thread in site selection is preference for places whose public history is incomplete or contested: uranium weapons production (Fernald), industrial contamination remediated into public land (Krejci Dump), segregated CCC labor (Hocking Hills, Palo Duro, Mount Airy), forced removal of functioning communities (Caesar Creek/New Burlington), systematic archaeological extraction without consent (Hopewell earthworks, Flint Ridge, Big Bone Lick). The shadow_history metadata field makes this layer structurally present for every site.

**Indigenous earthworks cluster.** Eight sites document the Hopewell and Adena traditions: Newark Earthworks, Octagon Earthworks, Seip Earthworks, Hopewell Culture National Historical Park, Fort Hill State Memorial, Flint Ridge State Memorial, Arc of Appalachia (Serpent Mound context), and Shawnee Lookout. This forms a coherent documentary project within the larger index.

**Ecological rarity and distinctiveness.** Sites are chosen for biological significance beyond public profile: old-growth forest (Fort Hill, Dinsmore Woods, St. Anne's, Glen Helen), globally rare oak savanna (Oak Openings), endemic flora (Red River Gorge's Solidago albopilosa, Edge of Appalachia's limestone prairie), Great Lakes coastal marsh (Magee Marsh, Ford Marsh, Pointe Mouillee, Sterling), and prairie remnants (Fernald, Killdeer Plains).

**Managing agency diversity.** The index spans NPS, USFS, USFWS, state parks and nature preserves, county and municipal parks, DOE legacy sites, and private conservation preserves. No single management type dominates.

**Scale range.** Sites run from 63 acres (Parker Woods) to 125,000 acres (Big South Fork). Neighborhood parks and national parks appear on the same documentary register.

## Photographed Sites — Ecology and Photographic Notes

All sites below have images in the index. Sites are grouped by ecological/photographic character.

---

### Hopewell and Adena Earthworks

**Newark Earthworks** (OH, 206 acres, 9 images)
Former golf course undergoing prairie restoration within the largest geometric earthwork complex in the world. Photographic emphasis on the Great Circle's massive earth walls at close and middle distance, the contrast between managed turfgrass and enclosure geometry, and the physical scale of a structure built without draft animals or metal tools. The grass-and-sky compositions are necessarily spare; the earthworks do not read as dramatic landforms until you are inside them.

**Octagon Earthworks** (OH, 70 acres, 4 images)
Companion to Newark; same restoration context. The octagon-to-circle gateway passage offers strong geometric perspectives. Golf infrastructure still partially visible in the landscape transition.

**Seip Earthworks** (OH, 121 acres, 8 images)
Restored tallgrass prairie within enclosure walls on a Paint Creek outwash terrace. Big bluestem, switchgrass, and Indiangrass in full growth provide movement and texture. The earthwork embankments read as low ridges against open agricultural sky; the prairie subjects are more immediately photogenic than the earthwork geometry itself.

**Hopewell Culture National Historical Park** (OH, 1,170 acres, 9 images)
Mound City unit: a compact cluster of mounded enclosures on a Scioto River terrace. Subjects include earthwork profiles in low-angle light, the reconstructed mound as geometric form, and meadow-to-woodland edge transitions. The managed hay field context is less visually resolved than Seip.

**Fort Hill State Memorial** (OH, 1,300 acres, 7 images)
Old-growth mixed mesophytic forest on a Hopewell hilltop enclosure. Unlike the geometric earthwork sites, Fort Hill is primarily a forest subject — the enclosure walls are subsurface earthen ridges visible underfoot but not visually dominant. Subjects: canopy structure, gorge geology, Silurian dolomite in Baker Fork, bark texture, spring ephemerals. The old-growth character distinguishes it from any other Ohio earthwork site.

**Flint Ridge State Memorial** (OH, 533 acres, 4 images)
Mature hardwood forest over a 12,000-year-old chert quarry landscape. Quarry pit depressions have become vernal pools — the primary photographic subjects are these water-filled pits, the multicolored chert exposed at the surface, and the beech-maple canopy over altered ground.

**Shawnee Lookout** (OH, 2,179 acres, 5 images)
Ridge forest above the Ohio-Great Miami confluence with Hopewell earthwork remnants. Subjects include pawpaw groves with their broad tropical-scale leaves, river viewsheds from the ridge, and tallgrass prairie corridor remnants along trail.

**Arc of Appalachia** (OH, 7,000+ acres, 5 images)
Serpent Mound and Lynx Prairie. The effigy mound from the observation tower is the signature image; Lynx Prairie offers dry limestone prairie botanicals and cedar savanna compositions. The unglaciated plateau edge creates dramatic geological context absent from the Scioto Valley earthwork sites.

### Great Lakes Marsh and Shoreline

**Magee Marsh Wildlife Area** (OH, 2,202 acres, 8 images)
Premier spring warbler migration site on Lake Erie. The 1.3-mile boardwalk at close canopy level enables warbler photography at distances not possible in typical forest settings. Migration concentrations, marsh surface reflections, emergent vegetation structure, and shorebird activity on adjacent beach. The photographic context is unusually intimate for bird subjects.

**Ford Marsh Unit, Detroit River IWR** (MI, 485 acres, 4 images)
Coastal freshwater marsh at the River Raisin mouth. American lotus (Nelumbo lutea) in open water is the primary botanical subject, alongside emergent marsh edge and Great Lakes open water. AOC remediation context gives the site a recovery-documentation dimension.

**William C. Sterling State Park** (MI, 1,300 acres, 7 images)
Lake Erie beach, managed lagoons, and restored prairie. Only Michigan state park on Lake Erie. Subjects: shoreline, lagoon marsh edge, Monroe Power Plant cooling towers on the horizon as industrial counterpoint.

**Pointe Mouillee State Game Area** (MI, 4,040 acres, 13 images)
The largest freshwater coastal marsh restoration project in the Great Lakes. Shorebird concentrations, waterfowl in migration staging, the scale of the managed diked cell infrastructure, and the Big Banana disposal dike. The managed-system aesthetic — artificial impoundments, control structures, mown dike crests — is integral to the subject.

**Killdeer Plains Wildlife Area** (OH, 9,054 acres, 4 images)
Restored Sandusky Plains wetland and grassland complex. Fall sandhill crane migration staging (10,000+ cranes observed) is the primary event. Impoundment reflections, grassland birds, and the flat Sandusky Plains horizon.

### Gorge and Geological

**Hocking Hills State Park** (OH, 2,356 acres, 14 images)
Hemlock-dominated gorge; Canadian hemlock at its southern range limit. Rock shelter recesses (the Hocking sandstone cantilevers), waterfall long exposures, old-growth hemlock groves on moisture-dependent north-facing slopes, moss-covered sandstone, and cave interiors. One of the most photographically active sites in the index by image count.

**Red River Gorge Geological Area** (KY, 29,000 acres, 11 images)
Pennsylvanian sandstone arches, cliff faces, and gorge depth. Solidago albopilosa — the white-haired goldenrod found nowhere else on Earth — grows on sandstone ledges and rock shelter floors. Arch subjects (Sky Bridge, Nada Tunnel) alongside endemic flora close-up work and cliff-face botanical communities.

**New River Gorge National Park** (WV, 72,808 acres, 18 images)
Mixed mesophytic gorge, the second-highest image count in the index. Gorge vistas, hemlock-rhododendron understory in the cove communities, the New River, and coal town industrial remnants. The scale of the gorge and the layered human-industrial-ecological history make it the project's most complex single subject.

**John Bryan State Park / Clifton Gorge** (OH, 752 / 268 acres, 7 images)
Dolomite gorge with relict northern flora — Canadian yew, walking fern, white cedar — surviving in cool microclimate. Little Miami River subjects, gorge wall faces, and early mill ruins in the gorge corridor.

**Rocky Fork State Park** (OH, 3,464 acres, 5 images)
Silurian dolomite gorge with rare cliff flora. Sullivantia sullivantii grows on moist seeping dolomite faces; the plant's delicate habit against wet stone is the primary close subject. Rocky Fork Creek corridor.

**Johnson's Shut-Ins State Park** (MO, 8,916 acres, 7 images)
Ozark rhyolite river chutes: the Black River moving through Precambrian igneous rock. Water-in-rock compositions, rhyolite surface texture, pool reflections.

**Elephant Rocks State Park** (MO, 132 acres, 5 images)
Eroded granite boulders with lichen communities. Human scale against granite mass, lichen pattern macro, the boulder train from above. The Ozark granite is among the oldest exposed bedrock in the index at ~1.5 billion years.

**Palo Duro Canyon State Park** (TX, 29,182 acres, 13 images)
Texas panhandle canyon cutting through Permian and Triassic strata. Geological color layering — red, white, gray, ochre — is the dominant subject. Cottonwood riparian corridor at canyon floor, evening and morning light on canyon walls. Second-largest canyon in the US and the most geologically colorful site in the index.

**Caprock Canyon State Park** (TX, 15,313 acres, 11 images)
The Caprock Escarpment edge and the Texas State Bison Herd, last free-ranging southern plains bison lineage. Cliff-edge geology, the herd at range in shortgrass canyon, and the escarpment drop. Companion trip to Palo Duro.

### Old-Growth and Forest Interior

**Dinsmore Woods State Nature Preserve** (KY, 107 acres, 5 images)
Old-growth sugar maple, white oak, hackberry, and walnut unlogged since at least the 1830s, on a former plantation site with documented enslaved labor. Subjects: old-growth canopy structure, spring ephemerals (trillium, bloodroot, trout lily), deep hardwood forest floor. The plantation shadow history is documented in metadata and not photographically visible — the forest presents as simply intact, which is the layered point.

**St. Anne's Woods and Wetlands** (KY, 165 acres, 11 images)
E. Lucy Braun-documented old-growth American beech and Ohio River floodplain depression wetlands. Beech trunk subjects (smooth gray bark at scale), depression wetland reflections, floodplain forest light. One of the smallest sites with the longest ecological documentation history in the collection.

**Glen Helen Nature Preserve** (OH, 1,147 acres, 8 images)
Mixed mesophytic gorge with 400-year-old trees, beaver wetland, and Yellow Spring itself. Old-growth trunk subjects, gorge stream long exposures, spring color, raptor center context.

**Clifton Gorge State Nature Preserve** (OH, 268 acres, 5 images)
Gorge companion to John Bryan; relict northern flora. Often photographed in the same visit. Dolomite wall subjects, walking fern in rock crevices, the confined gorge river light.

### Prairie and Savanna

**Oak Openings Metropark** (OH, 5,000 acres, 9 images)
Globally rare oak savanna and sand barren; one-third of Ohio's endangered plant species in this region. Black oak savanna light and spacing, wild lupine (the endangered Karner blue butterfly's host), sand prairie botanical work. Visually unlike any other Ohio site — open, sandy, Mediterranean in character relative to surrounding landscape.

**Edge of Appalachia Preserve** (OH, 21,000 acres, 16 images)
Lynx Prairie (dry limestone prairie), cedar barrens, and old-growth forest on the Appalachian Escarpment. The highest image count among non-NPS Ohio sites. Botanical close work on prairie species, cedar savanna structure, geological cliff overlooks, Brush Creek valley floor. The diversity of communities within the preserve (prairie, forest, gorge, bluff) sustains multiple photographic approaches in a single visit.

**Fernald Preserve** (OH, 1,050 acres, 6 images)
Restored tallgrass prairie and wetlands on former nuclear weapons production land. The photographic tension is between ecological recovery (dickcissel in the switchgrass, bobolink in flight) and industrial legacy (interpretive signage, remediation infrastructure, the former FMPC silos). Documentary character more explicit here than anywhere else in the index.

**Wichita Mountains National Wildlife Refuge** (OK, 59,020 acres, 15 images)
Mixed-grass and shortgrass prairie with reintroduced bison herd (~650 animals) and longhorn cattle. Bison at range in granite inselberg landscape, shortgrass prairie scale, prairie dog towns, cross timbers edge. One of the few places in the index where large fauna are the primary subject.

**Charon's Garden Wilderness Area** (OK, 5,723 acres, 4 images)
Designated wilderness within WMNWR, no mechanized access. Precambrian granite outcrop surfaces, black-capped vireo habitat thicket, prairie expanse without infrastructure. Quieter and more isolated than the main refuge.

### Contamination, Remediation, and Industrial Legacy

**Krejci Dump, CVNP** (OH, 200 acres, 5 images)
Restored seasonal wetland on a former hazardous waste site; CERCLA remediation certified complete 2020. Restored sedge and wildflower meadow, interpretive infrastructure, seasonal wetland water. The ecological recovery is recent and visually incomplete — the restored meadow reads as new, not settled. Documentary character.

**Caesar Creek State Park** (OH, 7,530 acres, 4 images)
Army Corps reservoir impounded 1971-1978 over the drowned town of New Burlington. Reservoir shoreline, exposed foundations and structural remnants during drawdown periods, spillway infrastructure. The submerged town history is invisible except at low water, making photographic access to the shadow history condition-dependent.

**Cuyahoga Valley National Park** (OH, 32,950 acres, 8 images)
The Cuyahoga River corridor with industrial recovery context. Mixed deciduous forest, ravine waterfalls (Brandywine Falls the primary subject), bald eagle, and the river itself — now biologically recovered. The park's origin in the 1969 river fire is documented in metadata and influences how the river subjects read as evidence of either loss or recovery.

### Coastal and Tidal

**Sea Rim State Park** (TX, 15,109 acres, 4 images)
Gulf Coast tidal salt marsh and beach; Central Flyway migration corridor. Tidal marsh at the land-water horizon, chenier ridge vegetation, Gulf beach. The flat Gulf coastal plain and active oil infrastructure visible offshore distinguish this from any other PLI site.

---

### Cave and Karst

**Mammoth Cave National Park** (KY, 54,012 acres, 28 images)
The highest image count in the index. The world's longest known cave system with a complex surface landscape. Cave passage interiors (artificial lighting, formation close-work, passage depth and scale), the Green River corridor above ground, and the agricultural plateau at park margins. The cave subjects require artificial light and involve compositional problems distinct from all surface work in the collection.

### Urban and Municipal Preserves

**French Park** (OH, 275 acres, 12 images)
Cincinnati Parks; mixed mesophytic forest with Ordovician fossil beds exposed in creek. Fossil crinoids and brachiopods at macro scale, pawpaw thicket structure, creek corridor, Hopewell mound context. Accessible year-round; treated as a field site for geological close-up work.

**Mount Airy Forest** (OH, 1,459 acres, 3 images)
CCC-planted arboretum and urban forest; CCC Company 1505-C (segregated Black unit) built infrastructure 1935-42. Arboretum diversity, CCC stonework, urban canopy.

**Parker Woods** (OH, ~63 acres, 6 images)
Mature urban sugar maple-white oak-beech forest; spring ephemerals; invasive removal ongoing. The most intimate and neighborhood-scale site in the index.

**Otto Armleder Memorial Park** (OH, 347 acres, 4 images)
Ohio River floodplain bottomland; willow, cottonwood, boxelder; migratory bird stopover on the Ohio flyway. Floodplain flood-cycle subjects; bottomland forest light.

**Bender Mountain Nature Preserve** (OH, 130 acres, 6 images)
Steep Ohio River bluff with the best native wildflower diversity in Hamilton County. Spring ephemerals on slope, river viewshed from bluff edge, native flora close-up work.

---

### Great Lakes Regional

**Big Bone Lick State Historic Site** (KY, 525 acres, 8 images)
Active sulphur spring and salt lick complex where North American megafauna accumulated over millennia; 300 years of ongoing fossil extraction. The spring and lick themselves, sulphur mineral deposits, native grassland along Discovery Trail. The paleontological shadow history (bones removed since 1739 by French officers, Jefferson, and commercial collectors) is invisible in the current landscape.

**Clifty Falls State Park** (IN, 1,416 acres, 10 images)
Clifty Canyon and falls on Ordovician and Silurian sedimentary rock near the Ohio River at Madison. Waterfall subjects, canyon depth, chestnut oak and beech forest at canyon rim.
